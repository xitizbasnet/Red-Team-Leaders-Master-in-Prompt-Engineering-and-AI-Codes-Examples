# Email Templates Library

## Module 07: AI for Daily Productivity — Code and Examples

---

A comprehensive library of 30 email prompt templates covering every major business communication scenario. Each template is ready to copy, customize, and use with any AI assistant.

---

## How to Use This Library

1. Find the template that matches your scenario
2. Copy the prompt
3. Replace `[BRACKETED PLACEHOLDERS]` with your specific information
4. Paste into your AI tool
5. Review the output and personalize before sending

**Pro tip**: Save your most-used templates with your personal defaults pre-filled.

## Category A: Outreach and Networking (Templates 1-6)

### Template 1: Cold Outreach — Product/Service

```
Write a cold outreach email.

From: [YOUR NAME], [YOUR TITLE] at [YOUR COMPANY]
To: [RECIPIENT NAME], [THEIR TITLE] at [THEIR COMPANY]
Industry: [THEIR INDUSTRY]

What we offer: [PRODUCT/SERVICE — ONE SENTENCE]
Why them specifically: [PERSONALIZED REASON — RECENT NEWS, SHARED CONNECTION, ETC.]
Key value proposition: [PRIMARY BENEFIT IN ONE SENTENCE]
Social proof: [NOTABLE CLIENT OR RESULT]
Ask: [MEETING / DEMO / CALL — SPECIFIC AND LOW-COMMITMENT]

Requirements:
- Subject line that creates curiosity (not clickbait)
- Under 125 words total
- Personalized opening line (NOT about us)
- One clear value statement
- One piece of social proof
- Simple, easy-to-answer CTA
- No attachments or multiple links
- P.S. line with an additional hook (optional)
```

### Template 2: Cold Outreach — Networking / Relationship Building

```
Write a networking outreach email.

From: [YOUR NAME AND ROLE]
To: [RECIPIENT NAME AND ROLE]
How I know of them: [THEIR BLOG / TALK / MUTUAL CONNECTION / LINKEDIN POST]
What specifically impressed me: [SPECIFIC THING THEY DID OR SAID]
What we have in common: [SHARED INTEREST, INDUSTRY, CHALLENGE]
My ask: [COFFEE CHAT / ADVICE / INTRODUCTION — KEEP IT SMALL]
What I can offer them: [VALUE I BRING TO THE RELATIONSHIP]

Tone: Genuine, humble, respectful of their time.
Under 100 words. Make it feel human, not templated.
Do NOT use phrases like "pick your brain" or "thought leader."
```

### Template 3: Follow-Up After Meeting / Event

```
Write a follow-up email after meeting someone.

Met at: [EVENT / MEETING / CONFERENCE NAME]
Their name: [NAME AND TITLE]
What we discussed: [KEY TOPIC]
Something personal I remember: [DETAIL THAT SHOWS I WAS LISTENING]
Next step we agreed on: [IF ANY]
What I want to happen next: [SPECIFIC ASK]
Timeframe: Sending this [SAME DAY / NEXT DAY / WITHIN A WEEK]

Reference our conversation specifically.
Keep it warm and personal, not transactional.
Under 100 words.
```

### Template 4: Follow-Up — No Response (Sequence)

```
Write a follow-up email sequence for someone who has not responded.

Context: I originally emailed about [TOPIC] on [DATE].
Relationship: [COLD / WARM / EXISTING]
Original ask: [WHAT I ASKED FOR]

Write 3 follow-up emails:

FOLLOW-UP 1 (3-5 days after original):
- Friendly bump, add new value or information
- Under 75 words

FOLLOW-UP 2 (7-10 days after follow-up 1):
- Different angle or approach
- Offer an alternative (e.g., shorter meeting, async response)
- Under 75 words

FOLLOW-UP 3 (14 days after follow-up 2 — the breakup email):
- Gracefully close the loop
- Leave the door open for the future
- Make it easy for them to say "not now" without guilt
- Under 60 words
```

## Category B: Internal Communication (Templates 7-12)

### Template 7: Project Status Update

```
Write a project status update email.

Project: [NAME]
Reporting to: [STAKEHOLDERS]
Reporting period: [THIS WEEK / THIS SPRINT / THIS MONTH]
Overall status: [GREEN / YELLOW / RED]

Completed this period:
- [ITEM 1]
- [ITEM 2]
- [ITEM 3]

In progress:
- [ITEM] — [% COMPLETE] — Expected completion: [DATE]
- [ITEM] — [% COMPLETE] — Expected completion: [DATE]

Blocked/At risk:
- [ITEM] — Blocker: [WHAT] — Need from: [WHO]

Upcoming:
- [NEXT MAJOR MILESTONE AND DATE]

Format with clear headers and bullet points.
Lead with the status indicator (Green/Yellow/Red) and one-line summary.
Keep under 200 words — make it scannable.
```

### Template 8: Requesting Approval / Decision

```
Write an email requesting approval.

Requesting from: [NAME AND TITLE]
What needs approval: [SPECIFIC DECISION]
Deadline for decision: [DATE]

Background: [2-3 SENTENCES OF CONTEXT]

Options (if applicable):
Option A: [DESCRIPTION] — Cost: [X] — Timeline: [Y] — Risk: [Z]
Option B: [DESCRIPTION] — Cost: [X] — Timeline: [Y] — Risk: [Z]

My recommendation: [WHICH OPTION AND WHY]

Impact of delay: [WHAT HAPPENS IF THE DECISION IS NOT MADE BY DEADLINE]

Make the decision easy:
- Present clear options
- Include your recommendation with reasoning
- Make the default action obvious
- Offer to discuss if they have questions

End with: "If I do not hear back by [DATE], I will proceed with [DEFAULT]."
```

### Template 10: Delegating a Task

```
Write a delegation email.

Deadline: [DATE AND TIME]
Priority: [HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW]

### Template 11: Giving Constructive Feedback

```
Write an email providing constructive feedback.

To: [NAME AND ROLE]
About: [SPECIFIC BEHAVIOR, DELIVERABLE, OR INCIDENT]
When it happened: [DATE/CONTEXT]

The situation: [OBJECTIVE DESCRIPTION OF WHAT HAPPENED]
The impact: [HOW IT AFFECTED THE TEAM, PROJECT, OR OUTCOME]
What I would like to see instead: [SPECIFIC DESIRED BEHAVIOR]
Support I can offer: [TRAINING, MENTORING, RESOURCES]

Tone: Supportive, direct, growth-oriented.
Use the SBI framework (Situation, Behavior, Impact).
Acknowledge their strengths before addressing the area for improvement.
Offer to discuss in person — email may not be the full conversation.
End with confidence in their ability to improve.
```

### Template 12: Requesting Help or Resources

```
Write an email requesting help from another team/department.

To: [NAME AND ROLE/DEPARTMENT]
From: [YOUR NAME AND ROLE]
What I need: [SPECIFIC REQUEST]
Why: [CONTEXT AND BUSINESS JUSTIFICATION]
Timeline: [WHEN I NEED IT BY]
Level of effort for them: [ESTIMATED TIME/RESOURCES]
What I have already done: [SHOW YOU HAVE TRIED FIRST]
What happens if I do not get this: [IMPACT OF NOT HELPING]
What I can offer in return: [RECIPROCITY]

Tone: Respectful, not entitled. Acknowledge you are asking for their time.
Make it as easy as possible for them to say yes.
Be specific about what you need — vague requests get ignored.
```

## Category C: Client and Customer Communication (Templates 13-18)

### Template 13: New Client Welcome

```
Write a client welcome/onboarding email.

Client name: [NAME]
Company: [COMPANY]
Product/service purchased: [WHAT THEY BOUGHT]
Account manager: [YOUR NAME AND CONTACT INFO]
Contract start date: [DATE]

Include:
- Warm welcome and expression of excitement
- Confirmation of what they purchased
- Who their primary contact is (and backup)
- Next steps with timeline:
  1. [STEP 1 — TIMING]
  2. [STEP 2 — TIMING]
  3. [STEP 3 — TIMING]
- Important resources/links:
  - Support portal: [URL]
  - Documentation: [URL]
  - Emergency contact: [PHONE/EMAIL]
- What they can expect in the first 30 days

Tone: Professional, warm, reassuring. Make them feel they made the right choice.
```

Our service/product: [WHAT THEY USE]

```

### Template 15: Handling a Complaint / Issue

```
Write a customer complaint response email.

Customer: [NAME]
Issue: [WHAT WENT WRONG — FROM THEIR PERSPECTIVE]
Our fault: [YES / NO / PARTIALLY]
Severity: [MINOR INCONVENIENCE / SIGNIFICANT PROBLEM / CRITICAL]
What we can do: [RESOLUTION OPTIONS]
Compensation available: [IF APPLICABLE — REFUND, CREDIT, FREE SERVICE]

Structure:
1. Acknowledge and validate their frustration (do not be defensive)
2. Apologize sincerely (if it was our fault)
3. Explain what happened (briefly, no excuses)
4. Describe what we are doing to fix it (specific and immediate)
5. Describe what we are doing to prevent it in the future
6. Offer compensation (if appropriate)
7. Provide direct contact for further issues
8. Close with a commitment to their satisfaction

Tone: Empathetic, accountable, solution-focused.
Do NOT use phrases like "We apologize for any inconvenience."
Be specific and genuine.
```

### Template 16: Proposal Follow-Up

```
Write a follow-up email after sending a proposal.

Client: [NAME AND COMPANY]
Proposal sent: [DATE]
Proposal topic: [WHAT IT WAS FOR]
Proposal value: [AMOUNT IF APPLICABLE]
Decision timeline they mentioned: [IF KNOWN]
Their key concern: [IF KNOWN — PRICE, TIMELINE, SCOPE, ETC.]

Version 1: 3 days after sending (value-add follow-up)
- Reference the proposal
- Share an additional relevant resource or insight
- Offer to walk through any questions
- Do NOT be pushy

Version 2: 7-10 days after sending (direct check-in)
- Ask specifically if they had a chance to review
- Offer to modify or adjust based on feedback
- Suggest a brief call to discuss

Version 3: 14+ days after sending (decision prompt)
- Ask about their timeline
- Offer to address any remaining concerns
- Provide a gentle reason for urgency if applicable
```

### Template 17: Upsell / Cross-Sell

## Category D: Difficult / Sensitive Situations (Templates 19-24)

### Template 19: Delivering Bad News to a Client

```
Write an email delivering bad news.

To: [CLIENT NAME AND ROLE]
Bad news: [WHAT HAPPENED — E.G., MISSED DEADLINE, PRICE INCREASE, SERVICE ISSUE]
Cause: [HONEST, BRIEF EXPLANATION — NOT EXCUSES]
Impact on them: [SPECIFICALLY HOW THIS AFFECTS THEM]
Our plan: [WHAT WE ARE DOING ABOUT IT]
Timeline for resolution: [WHEN]
Compensation/goodwill: [IF APPLICABLE]

Rules:
- State the bad news in the first or second sentence (do not bury it)
- Take responsibility (no passive voice like "mistakes were made")
- Focus 70% of the email on the solution, 30% on the problem
- Offer a call to discuss (some news is better delivered by voice)
- Be specific about next steps and dates
```

### Template 20: Saying No / Declining a Request

```
Write an email politely declining a request.

Request from: [NAME AND ROLE]
What they asked for: [THE REQUEST]
Why I am declining: [HONEST REASON — CAPACITY, PRIORITY, SCOPE, ETC.]
Alternative I can offer: [IF ANY]
Redirect: [SOMEONE ELSE WHO MIGHT HELP, IF APPLICABLE]

Rules:
- Be direct — say no clearly, do not leave ambiguity
- Be kind — acknowledge their need and validate the request
- Be brief — long explanations sound like excuses
- Offer an alternative if possible
- Do not apologize excessively (one "I appreciate you thinking of me" is enough)
- Leave the relationship intact

Under 100 words.
```

### Template 21: Addressing a Misunderstanding

```
Write an email clearing up a misunderstanding.

With: [NAME AND ROLE]
The misunderstanding: [WHAT THEY THINK vs WHAT IS ACTUALLY TRUE]
How it arose: [IF KNOWN — MISCOMMUNICATION, MISSING CONTEXT]
What I need to clarify: [THE CORRECT INFORMATION]
What we need to do now: [NEXT STEPS TO RESOLVE]

Rules:
- Do not blame them for the misunderstanding
- State facts, not accusations
- Use "I" statements ("I may not have been clear" vs "You misunderstood")
- Propose the path forward collaboratively
- Suggest a call if the topic is complex
```

### Template 22: Requesting Payment / Collections

```
Write a payment reminder email.

Client: [NAME AND COMPANY]
Invoice number: [NUMBER]
Invoice amount: [AMOUNT]
Due date: [DATE]
Days overdue: [NUMBER]
Our relationship: [NEW / ESTABLISHED / LONG-TERM]

Write 3 progressively firmer versions:

VERSION 1 (1-7 days overdue): Friendly reminder
- Assume it was an oversight
- Include invoice details
- Provide payment methods
- Under 75 words

VERSION 2 (14-21 days overdue): Firm but professional
- Reference the previous reminder
- State the importance of timely payment
- Ask if there is an issue preventing payment
- Under 100 words

VERSION 3 (30+ days overdue): Final notice
- Formal tone
- Clear consequences (late fees, service suspension, collections)
- Final deadline
- Offer to arrange a payment plan
- Under 125 words
```

### Template 23: Resignation Email

```
Write a resignation email.

To: [MANAGER NAME AND TITLE]
CC: [HR CONTACT IF APPLICABLE]
My position: [TITLE]
Last working day: [DATE]
Notice period: [WEEKS]
Reason: [OPTIONAL — ONLY IF YOU WANT TO SHARE]

Include:
- Clear statement of resignation and last day
- Gratitude (specific, not generic — mention something meaningful)
- Offer to help with transition
- Positive forward-looking closing

Do NOT include:
- Complaints or grievances
- Details about the new opportunity (unless asked)
- Passive-aggressive comments
- Over-the-top flattery

Tone: Professional, grateful, concise. This email becomes part of your
permanent record. Write it as the classiest version of yourself.
Under 150 words.
```

## Quick Reference: Email Best Practices

Before sending any AI-generated email, run through this checklist:

```
PRE-SEND CHECKLIST:
[ ] Subject line is clear and specific (not vague or clickbait)
[ ] Recipient name and details are correct
[ ] Tone matches the relationship and situation
[ ] One clear ask or CTA (not multiple competing requests)
[ ] Length is appropriate (shorter is almost always better)
[ ] No sensitive information that should not be in email
[ ] Attachments are actually attached (if mentioned)
[ ] Reply-all is appropriate (or should this be direct?)
[ ] Spelling and grammar reviewed
[ ] Read it from the recipient's perspective — would YOU want to receive this?
[ ] Removed any AI artifacts or placeholder text
[ ] CC list is appropriate (not too many, not missing anyone critical)
```
